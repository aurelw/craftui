A plug and play solution for large-scale touch interfaces.

CraftUI

CraftUI lets you create touch interfaces on physical surfaces and was initially developed for glass surfaces like public show windows. While this is a very basic concept, we believe that CraftUI distinguishes itself from many other multi touch technologies in a way that it is a much more practical solution for many applications.

Introduction

In early 2015 we started to implement a touch interface for the public display window of /dev/lol, a hackerspace in Linz, Austria. We wanted to give people a way to interact with our projects and reach out to simple pedestrian which would otherwise not be interested in what we are doing. A complicated interface, which would have any learning curve, and which wouldn't be self-explanatory was therefore out of the question. Therefore, it worked best for us to draw some virtual buttons on the glass, which people would touch while standing on the street.

Our experience with other 3D vision applications tells us, that there are many pitfalls and details which can render a great application completely worthless in practice. With computer vision application targeting end-users without expert knowledge, calibration processes, stability and robustness pose big problems. With CraftUI we have the goal to create a true practical application which addresses these issues properly. We try to keep CraftUI a simple tool and on the other hand do this simple things just perfectly right.

Process

Setting up a glass surface as a touch interface should be easy for anybody. Therefore, we created a very tactile, one-click process to specify and calibrate the UI! Calibration in general is often painful and tedious. What CraftUI does, it takes calibration, and combines it with a creative process of designing an artistic UI. Actually, calibration can be fun.

"Craft" as in "arts and craft". We take several sheets of colored paper, one rectangle with a known size. Then, we cut out the shapes of our buttons and tape everything flat to the glass surface.

The program then takes one RGBD (RGB + depth) image and extracts the rectangular marker. It then searches for other objects with similar color and creates a hitbox for each match. Then we encircle the paper markers and draw additional information or artwork. Window colors or white board markers work best. After the buttons are well marked, we remove all paper from the glass.

The program then does some additional auto calibration to suppress any noise introduces introduced by the paintings. The buttons are now ready to use.

Interoperability, MQTT and Usescases

CraftUI can publishes MQTT messages which we use control IoT devices. At our hackerspace, the first permanent installation of CraftUI, someone can control the color of multiple lights, post a message to IRC, start a disco ball, control a browser game, get feedback messages on an LED display or show real time public transport data. We also experimented with a beamer and projection mapping setup. The most popular interaction however is a small, MQTT controlled Maneki-neko, a Japanese lucky cat, which starts to wave when a certain button is touched.

In the fist 8 months, tho the window is located in a rather quiet street, we counted about 1000 users. In general, feedback was very positive so far. We want CraftUI to be a simple tool which just publishes UI events and be part of art installations, games, interactive shopping windows, etc.

Why Google Tango

We understand, that we won't use the key features Google Tango provides, like it's SLAM and tracking features and just make use of a stationary depth camera. However, because of the fast and easy calibration process and the ad hoc 'wiring' with MQTT, we beliefe that CraftUI is ideal for temporary and spontaneous setups. Therefore a mobile platform including a 3D camera gives many benefits. Mounting a separate camera and running cables to laptop is also cumbersome when creating a temporary installation. Having everything integrated in a small and lightweight form factor adds a lot to the practicability.
